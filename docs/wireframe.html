— design choices, and why —

One list. Three sections.

Today, This week, Later. No projects, tags, priorities, or filters. The user opens the app and the answer to "what do I do?" is the first thing they see.

Plain language only.

"HR is waiting" instead of "High priority." "Due in 3 weeks" instead of "May 31, 2026." The app talks to the user the way a thoughtful friend would.

The source is always shown.

Tiny, lowercase. Trust comes from "I can see where this came from." Without it, every item feels like an AI guess. With it, every item is verifiable in one tap.

Swipe to snooze. Tap to finish.

Two gestures. That's the whole interaction model. No long-press menus, no edit screens, no "are you sure" dialogs.

The empty state is the hero.

"You're clear" is the moment the product delivers on its promise. Most apps treat empty states as afterthoughts. Here, it's the destination.

Quiet by default.

Cream paper, ink, one warm accent. No red badges, no alerts, no urgency theater. The app you check should not look like the apps you're escaping.

"Later" is collapsed.

Things waiting next month exist, but the user doesn't have to look at them. Knowing they're held safely is enough — that's the second-brain feeling.

One add button. One sentence.

"Add something on your mind." Type or speak in any words. The AI parses the deadline and source. The user never picks a category.
